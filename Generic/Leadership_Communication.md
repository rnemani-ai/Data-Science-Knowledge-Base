# 45. How Do You Prioritize Multiple ML Projects?

## Interview Answer

I don't prioritize projects based on which model is the most interesting to build. I prioritize based on business impact, urgency, feasibility and strategic alignment.

I usually work with Product Managers and business stakeholders to understand expected business value, implementation effort, dependencies and risks. This helps ensure the team spends time on initiatives that create the highest return for the business.

A technically impressive project with little business impact is rarely the right priority.

---

## My Approach

I typically evaluate projects based on:

- Business impact
- Strategic alignment
- Customer value
- Engineering effort
- Data availability
- Technical risk
- Time to value

When multiple projects have similar impact, I usually prioritize the one that can deliver value faster.

---

## Enterprise Perspective

Suppose there are two projects:

Project A

- Expected revenue increase: $5M
- Requires six months of engineering effort

Project B

- Expected cost savings: $2M
- Can be deployed in six weeks

Rather than making the decision independently, I would present the trade-offs to stakeholders and recommend the option that best aligns with current business priorities.

---

## Common Follow-up Questions

### What framework do you use?

I often use an Impact vs Effort framework together with business risk and strategic importance.

### Who should make the final decision?

Prioritization should be a collaborative decision involving Product, Engineering and Business stakeholders.

# 46. How Do You Handle Conflicting Stakeholder Priorities?

## Interview Answer

Conflicting priorities are common in enterprise environments because different teams optimize for different objectives.

My role is to understand each stakeholder's goals, identify common ground and make trade-offs transparent rather than trying to satisfy everyone.

I focus discussions around business impact, measurable outcomes and organizational priorities instead of personal opinions.

---

## My Approach

- Listen to each stakeholder's objectives.
- Understand the underlying business problem.
- Quantify trade-offs.
- Align decisions with organizational goals.
- Document assumptions and decisions.
- Keep communication transparent.

---

## Enterprise Perspective

For example:

Marketing wanted higher Recall to identify more customers for retention campaigns.

Operations wanted higher Precision because their team had limited capacity.

Rather than choosing one side, we evaluated multiple thresholds and selected the one that maximized retained customers while staying within operational capacity.

The decision was driven by data rather than opinions.

---

## Common Follow-up Questions

### What if stakeholders still disagree?

I recommend running experiments or pilot deployments so that data informs the decision.

---

## Senior DS Insight

The best way to resolve disagreements is to replace opinions with evidence.

# 47. Tell Me About A Time You Pushed Back On A Stakeholder

## Interview Answer

There have been situations where stakeholders wanted to deploy a model immediately because offline metrics looked promising.

Rather than saying no, I explained the risks, including limited validation, potential drift and uncertainty around production performance.

I proposed a phased rollout with monitoring and success criteria instead of a full deployment.

This approach reduced business risk while still allowing the organization to realize value quickly.

---

## My Approach

When pushing back, I try to:

- Understand the motivation.
- Explain the risks objectively.
- Offer alternative solutions.
- Focus on business impact rather than technical concerns.
- Support recommendations with evidence.

---

## Enterprise Perspective

I've learned that stakeholders are much more receptive when I replace:

"We shouldn't do this."

with

"Here's a lower-risk approach that still achieves your objective."

---

## Common Follow-up Questions

### Have stakeholders ever disagreed?

Absolutely.

Healthy debate is part of building successful products.

My responsibility is to make recommendations based on data while remaining open to feedback.

---

They make them constructive.

# 48. How Do You Influence Without Authority?

## Interview Answer

Influence comes from credibility, collaboration and communication rather than job title.

I build trust by understanding stakeholder objectives, communicating clearly, involving teams early and consistently delivering reliable results.

People are much more likely to support recommendations when they understand the reasoning behind them.

---

## My Approach

- Build relationships early.
- Listen before proposing solutions.
- Speak in business language.
- Share data and evidence.
- Involve stakeholders throughout the project.
- Deliver consistently.

---

## Enterprise Perspective

One lesson I've learned is that involving stakeholders during problem definition leads to significantly higher adoption than presenting a completed solution at the end of the project.

People support what they help create.

---

## Common Follow-up Questions

### What's the biggest challenge?

Balancing different priorities while maintaining alignment on the overall business objective.

---

## Senior DS Insight

Influence isn't about convincing people.

It's about helping people make better decisions together.
